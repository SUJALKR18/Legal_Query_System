\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Problem Statement}

The Indian legal system has hundreds of laws, thousands of sections, and an ever-changing set of amendments to those laws. These laws are mostly complicated, and an average person has very little or no knowledge about his/her legal rights due to this high complexity of laws. It is very difficult for a normal person to have a quick and straightforward response to a legal question about property, marriage, criminal behavior, or digital privacy.

The available resources of legal information on the internet, such as government websites, legal databases or bare acts in PDF format, all of them use a legal language which is far beyond the understanding of a normal person. So in this case, there is a need for an easy to use and intelligent tool that can extract information from these documents and provide quick and east to understand answers to users.


\subsection{Proposed Solution}

To solve this problem, the \textbf{Legal Query System} is an online AI assistant tool that makes it easier for the user to ask a legal question and receive a meaningful answer that has an appropriate citation from legal documents, which is necessary if you want to use the answer in any type of court proceeding.

You can ask questions such as "What happens to someone when they break an agreement?" or "What are my rights after I am arrested?" and get a meaningful, clear, and well-defined answer with proper citations: Act number, section number, and the portion of the relevant clause from the applicable legislation.

This system uses a modern artificial intelligence method of Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), which guaranties that all answers provided are based on real-life legal documents rather than AI-based. This ensures that the system is accurate and reliable.

\subsection{What is RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation)?}

RAG is a technique that uses two ideas to make AI systems more reliable: 

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Retrieval} --- When a user asks a question, before answering the question, the system first searches through a knowledge base and retrieves the most relevant sections that resemble the user's query.
  \item \textbf{Generation} --- The sections which are retrieved by the systems are then passed into Large Language Model (LLM), which is used as context by LLM to generate accurate results.
\end{enumerate}

The main advantages of RAG over a normal AI chatbot is that the AI does not rely on its own memory to respond to user's query. Instead, it always refers to the original documents. This helps prevent hallucinations of AI or inaccurate responses when dealing with legal matters.

\section{PDF Ingestion Pipeline}

To successfully respond to legal requests, the system first must analyze and comprehend all uploaded legal documents. The process used for this is referred to as the PDF Ingestion Pipeline, in which various components are executed on the raw existing legal PDFs to prepare an appropriate searchable database that is ready for subsequent use by the Artificial Intelligence (AI) system. The pipeline is executed only once (or when there are newly uploaded documents) to establish the knowledge base for the RAG system.

\subsection{Text Extraction}

The initial phase of the PDF Ingestion Pipeline will consist of Extracting Readable Text from the PDF Files. In India, legal documents are almost always provided as a PDF format, which have formatted text, include headers and footers, page numbers and may be formatted as multiple columns

To extract the text from PDF documents our system utilizes the \textbf{PyPDF2} library in the Python programming language to open each PDF file and retrieve all text from each page in each PDF document. The extracted text will be stored as a plain text file for subsequent processing. The extracted text will also include a page marker denoting which page it originated (e.g., "PAGE 1" or "PAGE 2") to identify where each piece of extracted text belongs by page number.

% \begin{infobox}
% \textbf{What happens here:} A PDF file like \textit{indian\_contract\_act\_1872.pdf} goes in, and a clean text file \textit{indian\_contract\_act\_1872.txt} comes out, containing all the text from every page of the PDF.
% \end{infobox}

\subsection{Metadata Detection}

There needs to be sufficient identification of legal documents, this means that the type of act and also the ~Year it was established. Our system automatically detects metadata from the filename of each PDF.

The filename is processed using the following algorithm:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Replaces underscores found in the filename with spaces (e.g., \textit{indian\_contract\_act} becomes \textit{Indian Contract Act}).
  \item The last four digits in the filename are identified as a Year (e.g., \textit{1872}).
  \item The remaining part is title-cased to create a clean, readable Act name.
  \item For special cases (like the Aadhaar Act or the Constitution), a manual mapping is used to get the exact correct name.
\end{itemize}

This metadata (act name/year) will be attached to every chunk of text extracted from the specific document so that when AI quotes a source, it will quote the full act name with Year, e.g., \textit{``Indian Contract Act, 1872, Section 73''} instead of just a filename.
\subsection{Legal Text Chunking}

This is one of the most important and carefully designed parts of the pipeline. Legal documents are very long --- sometimes hundreds of pages. An AI model cannot process an entire document at once, so we need to break it into smaller, meaningful pieces called \textbf{``chunks.''} However, the way we chunk the text matters a lot --- we cannot just split it arbitrarily, or we would lose the meaning of legal sections.

\subsubsection{Document-Based Chunking Strategy}

Our chunking strategy is specifically designed for the structure of Indian legal documents. Indian Acts and laws follow a well-defined hierarchy:

\begin{highlightbox}
\textbf{Typical structure of an Indian legal document:} \textbf{Act Name} $\rightarrow$ multiple \textbf{Chapters} (e.g., Chapter I, Chapter II\ldots) $\rightarrow$ each Chapter contains multiple \textbf{Sections} (e.g., Section 1, Section 2\ldots) $\rightarrow$ each Section contains the actual legal text, definitions, provisions, and clauses.
\end{highlightbox}

Our system exploits this natural structure to create intelligent chunks through the following steps:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Raw Text Cleaning:} Before chunking, the extracted text is cleaned up. PDF artifacts like gazette headers, page markers, excessive whitespace, and formatting noise are removed.
  \item \textbf{Section-Based Splitting:} The system uses pattern-matching to identify section boundaries. It looks for patterns like ``1. Short title and commencement'' or ``33A. Penalty for fraud'' --- a number (optionally followed by a letter), a period, and then the section title.
  \item \textbf{Chapter Tracking:} As the system reads through the text, it also watches for chapter headers (like ``CHAPTER IV --- OF THE PERFORMANCE OF CONTRACTS''). Whenever it finds a chapter header, it updates the current chapter label.
  \item \textbf{Preamble Handling:} Any text that appears before the first section (such as the preamble, definitions, or introductory statements) is captured as a separate ``Preamble'' chunk.
  \item \textbf{Large Section Splitting:} If a section exceeds 3,000 characters, the system further splits it at sentence boundaries, looking for full stops or semicolons within a 1,500-character window.
  \item \textbf{Fallback for Non-Standard Documents:} If a document does not follow standard section numbering, the system falls back to pure sentence-boundary splitting, creating sequential parts labelled ``Part 1'', ``Part 2'', etc.
  \item \textbf{Short Section Filtering:} Very short text fragments (less than 70 characters) are automatically skipped, as these are typically table-of-contents entries or formatting artifacts.
\end{enumerate}

Each chunk is stored as a structured record containing both the text content and its associated metadata --- the Act name, year, source filename, chapter, section number, and section title.

\begin{infobox}
\textbf{Example Chunk:} \textbf{Act:} Indian Contract Act, 1872 \quad\textbf{Chapter:} VI --- Of the Consequences of Breach of Contract \quad \textbf{Section:} 73 --- Compensation for loss or damage caused by breach \quad \textbf{Text:} ``When a contract has been broken, the party who suffers by such breach is entitled to receive, as compensation\ldots''
\end{infobox}

\subsection{Vector Store Construction}
The last stage of processing the legal documents is to create a searchable collection of these documents based on their meaning rather than just keywords, and this is done by converting each chunk of text into a \textbf{vector embedding}, which is an array of numeric values representing the semantic meaning of the original text. The embeddings for the law documents will be stored in a \textbf{vector database}.





% Once all the legal documents have been extracted, cleaned, and chunked, the next step is to make them searchable by meaning rather than by keywords. This is done by converting each text chunk into a \textbf{vector embedding} --- a list of numbers that represents the meaning of the text --- and storing them in a \textbf{vector database}.

Our system uses the following components:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Sentence Transformers (all-MiniLM-L6-v2):} An AI model that was pre-trained and which can encode any piece of text into a 384-dimensional vector. Texts which are similar meanings will have close mathematical value.
  \item \textbf{ChromaDB:} A vector database that stores all of the embeddings and related metadata. ChromaDB has been optimised for rapid similarity searches using cosine similarity as the method of calculating distance.
  
  
  % A vector database that stores all the embeddings and their associated metadata. It is optimised for fast similarity searches using \textbf{cosine similarity} as the distance metric.
\end{itemize}

Each of the chunks will be embedded in batches of 32 to improve efficiency and then stored in ChromaDB in batches of 100. A persistent copy of the resulting vector database will be saved to disk, so it will only need to be computed once when the database is created. Afterwards, the vector database will be ready for use without having to be rebuilt every time the application is started.

\section{RAG Query Pipeline}

When a user inquires about a legal matter, the legal question is processed through the RAG Query Pipeline, which finds the most similar legal content in the vector database based on the cosine similarity and provides the AI model with this content, from which reasoning is performed and an appropriately cited and structured response is returned to the user.


\subsection{Semantic Retrieval}


The process of answering a legal question involves first locating the most relevant pieces of the legal database. The practice of locating relevant pieces within the legal database is known as \textbf{semantic retrieval}. Semantic retrieval is a form of retrieval that queries to locate results that are semantically related to the user query, rather than using just words.



When a user types a question (for example, \textit{``What is the punishment for theft?''}), the system:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item The user's question is then converted from natural language to a vector embedding by the same Sentence Transformer model which was used during ingestion.

  \item The top 6 most similar chunks (top-k nearest neighbor rate) based on semantic similarity are returned, with similarity calculated using cosine distance through the ChromaDB vector store.
  \item Returns these 6 chunks along with their metadata (Act name, section, chapter) and their similarity scores.
\end{enumerate}

The AI model uses this 6 piece collection of content to provide adequate context for an accurate answer to the user's question, as a legal question can refer to multiple act sections or multiple acts. 

\subsection{Context Formatting}

The AI model cannot use the retrieved chunks just as a singular string of text but it   must first properly organize the data from the 6 pieces into key sections containing labelled headings.
\begin{itemize}
  \item The \textbf{Act name} (e.g., ``Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita, 2023'')
  \item The \textbf{Section number and title} (e.g., ``Section 303 --- Theft'')
  \item The \textbf{Chapter} (e.g., ``CHAPTER XVII --- Of Offences Against Property'')
  \item The \textbf{Relevance score} (e.g., ``Relevance: 89.34\%'')
\end{itemize}

All chunks are then joined together as well-formated block of text which is then read and referenced by AI model.
\subsection{LLM Generation}

we are using  \textbf{Google Gemini 2.0 Flash} for the core intelligence of the system, accessed through the \textbf{Vertex AI} platform. This model will process the formatted context with the user’s query and generate a natural, readable answer. 

We used following \textbf{system prompt} to guide the LLM: 
\begin{itemize}
  \item First explain the answer in very simple, everyday language.
  \item Then provide the formal legal explanation with proper citations (Act name, Section number, and exact clause text).
  \item Never make up information --- if the provided context does not contain the answer, it should honestly say so.
  \item For non-legel conversation(like greeting), respond naturally without legel details.
  \item Always include a legal disclaimer at the end of legal answers.
\end{itemize}

The LLM has the ability to have multiple turns to a conversation, will keep up with the last 6 messages of the conversation that can assist users with their follow-up questions to what was previously provided to them. The LLM has a temperature of 0.2 (a significantly lower number) it responds with specific and consistent responses, which is critical in the area of law. 

\subsection{Source Filtering}

All retrieved chunks are not equally relevant, so to handle this, the system applies a \textbf{relevance threshold} of \textbf{0.45 (45\%)}; any chunk with a similarity score below the threshold will be filtered out and not shown to the user as a citation.


\begin{itemize}
  \item If a user is asking a legitimate question of law, only relevant sources will be shown
  \item If a user is casually chatting (like saying ``hello''), then irrelevant legal citations will not be provided.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Response Construction}

The last step is building and sending the response to the user. There will be two components of the response
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{The Answer:} The LLM will produce a Natural Language Answer that includes a simple response, a Legal Reasoning response and sources embedded with the answer as Natural Language.

  \item \textbf{The Source Citations:} A list of filtered relevant source documents will be shown to the user. Each entry will have the Act name, Section, Chapter and year, a cleaned excerpt of the source text plus the similarity score.
\end{enumerate}

This dual response design allows the user interface to display the answer prominently and provide expandable source citations for the user to verify them. Like a legal brief contains footnotes.

\section{System Architecture}

The Legal Query System follows a \textbf{three-tier architecture} with clearly separated responsibilities.

\subsection{Backend Architecture}

The backend consists of two servers running simultaneously:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{RAG API Server (Python / Flask --- Port 5000):} The RAG pipeline runs on this server. It reveals a /api/query endpoint that takes a question from the user and prints the answer generated by AI with references. It also offers an /api/ingest endpoint to cause re-ingestion of PDFs, and a /api/health endpoint to check health. The RAG pipeline is also loaded as a singleton (loaded once and reused on every request) to avoid the cost of loading models multiple times.
  

  \item \textbf{Application Server (Node.js / Express --- Port 3001):} user authentication, session management, and access to the RAG API. It is also linked to a MongoDB database to store user accounts and chat sessions. When a user makes a query using the frontend, this server authenticates the user, directs the query to the RAG API, receives a response, logs the conversation in the database, and sends the result to the frontend.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Frontend Architecture}

The frontend is developed with React.js (the Vite build tool is fast to develop with) and has four main pages:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Login Page:} Gives the chance to existing users to log in using their email and password.
  \item \textbf{Signup Page:} This is where new users can create an account with their name, email, and password.
  \item \textbf{Dashboard:} Shows every chat session of the user, in reverse order of most recent. Users can create new sessions, see previous ones, categorize queries as solved, or delete sessions.

  \item \textbf{Chat Page:} It is the primary interaction page where the user enters his/her legal query and gets answers in an AI-generated form with a collapsible reference list. The chat interface has multi-turn chat and a legal warning displayed with every reply.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Authentication \& Session Management}

The system adopts secure user authentication based on:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{JWT (JSON Web Tokens):} Once a user has been authenticated, a signed token is issued by the server and is used locally by the frontend. With each further request, this token is included to verify the identity of the user.
  \item \textbf{bcrypt Password Hashing:} User passwords are not stored in plain text. They are hashed with a salt factor of 12 which makes them virtually impossible to reverse-engineer.

  \item \textbf{Chat Sessions:} Conversations of each user are stored as individual sessions in MongoDB, including a complete history of messages and sources. Users have the ability to go back to past sessions to revisit past legal queries and answers.
\end{itemize}

\section{Future Scope}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Multi-Language Support:} Extending the system to languages like Hindi and other languages so it can be useful for a broad audience.
  \item \textbf{Case Law Integration:} Adding the judgments of the Supreme Court of India and judgments of high courts to the knowledge base so that our system can use and cite those judgments while giving answers to the users.
  \item \textbf{Hybrid Search:} using both semantic (vector) search and keyword-based (BM25) search to return better results for searches that contain specific terms or section numbers.
  \item \textbf{Voice Interface:} adding speech-to-text and text-to-speech technology so that users can ask a question orally, which is beneficial for users who don’t want to type their question into the system.
  \item \textbf{Document Upload:} enabling users to upload their own documents so that it can be analyzed and answered accordingly.
  \item \textbf{Advanced Citation Linking:} Chat response provides the link, which is clickable and which directly highlights the cited section text on the original PDF document.


  \item \textbf{Admin Dashboard:} The creation of an Administrative Panel for the purpose of tracking usage statistics, managing legal documents, and viewing analytics on the legal topics most frequently asked about by users.
  \item \textbf{Fine-Tuned Embeddings:} Training custom models specifically on Indian legal text, thus allowing the user to obtain the best legal search results possible.
\end{itemize}
